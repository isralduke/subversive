The Influences Upon The Art

These exert influence in such a way that the art itself is not merely subject to spirit, in generic terms, but to the living spirit in the Bible.

Illuminated Manuscripts

In the middle ages, scribes recopied manuscripts and classical texts for their patrons, very often adding touches to beautify the text, such as illustrations and decorative elements, signifying the love of the text, the material under their hands. This arrangement meant that many books were created over the course of their lifetimes. These texts were Bibles, classical texts, stories, and collections of knowledge. The illuminated manuscripts which came from this are one of the main ingredients for the inspiration behind Subversive Series.

Constructivism & Suprematism

The early 20th century saw an explosion of non-representational art in Europe. This was used in many ways, either as political tools to institute governmental control over all aspects of life, artistic experiments to expand artistic representation away from nature, and possibly even as a wartime weapon. The geometrical systems evident in Constructivism and Suprematism are very strong influences upon the Subversive Series.

Wassily Kandinsky

Wassily Kandinsky was a Russian lawyer who became a painter and later a teacher at the Bauhaus. His work was heavy influenced by his spirituality, as evident in his book Concerning the Spiritual in Art (Uber das Geistige in der Kunst). He promoted the idea that art should be liberated from Nature in order to promote the spirit, as a reaction against the encroachment of materialism upon art and society. In the same book mentioned above, he stated that art’s purpose was to feed the soul, and that artists should honor this their primary duty to society.

C.S. Lewis

CS Lewis was an Oxford professor and Christian apologist who wrote a great many books explaining the Christian faith and novels as allegories of the Christian faith. In his own works, Lewis uses many lessons that he himself learns from earlier influences such as Greek and Norse mythology, and the Romantic poets. Lewis saw his own love for literature, writing, as a tool by which Christ may draw a person to Himself.
